Accessibility overview

What is web accessibility?

Web accessibility means that people with disabilities can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with the Web, and that they can contribute to the Web.

Knowing about web accessibility involves a combination of things, including how people use assistive technology, how different impairments affect the use of websites, and how web-standards enable a consistent approach across websites.

Who does accessibility benefit?

Accessibility is something that will be relevant to everyone at some point in their lives. Whereas some people have recognised disabilities that permanently affect the way they use technology, others may experience a temporary loss of ability at some stage. Age can also be a contributing factor, with people experiencing a drop in visual and hearing acuity in later life.

There are many reasons why someone might find the web a challenging environment. However, there are many things you can do to ensure that Sky's website delivers an inclusive experience for everyone.

People with cognitive disabilities

Summary: The range of cognitive and learning disabilities is extraordinarily diverse. Conditions such as Dyslexia, Autism and Attention Deficit Disorder are all encompassed within this group.

General advice: People with cognitive disabilities often benefit from being able to customise content. For example, changing colour schemes, line length or controlling animated content. Keep page layouts across the website consistent, and make sure that navigation mechanisms are clear.

People with restricted mobility

Summary: People with restricted mobility often rely on devices other than a mouse to control their computer. Devices vary depending on the ability of each individual, but typically include keyboards, speech recognition, eye tracking, or head/mouth wands.

General advice: Most non-mouse devices are supported by ensuring that all aspects of the website can be used with only a keyboard. Provide clear visual indicators for the current location of keyboard focus on the page, and use skip links to provide easy shortcuts for bypassing large chunks of content on the page.

Deaf and hearing impaired people

Summary: People who are born profoundly (completely) deaf may have British Sign Language (BSL) as their primary language. Others may lose some or all of their hearing at some point during their lives, and in some cases may benefit from wearing a hearing aid.

General advice: Deaf or hearing impaired people will find audio content the biggest challenge. Provide transcripts and captions for the multimedia content, and use clear and simple language for the benefit of people who have BSL as their primary (and English as their secondary) language.

Blind and partially sighted people

Summary: People who have little or no usable sight may use a screen reader to translate on-screen information into synthetic speech or electronic Braille. Partially sighted people may use screen magnification software to enlarge content by as much as x32 magnification. People with mild conditions such as colour blindness are also encompassed within this group.

General advice: Blind and partially sighted people benefit from consistent page layouts and logical ordering of content. Ensure that text can be resized easily and consider offering a choice of alternative colour schemes.

Why is accessibility important?

Accessibility is a legal responsibility

In Great Britain accessibility is a legal requirement. The Equality Act (2010) in the UK, and Disability Discrimination Act (1995) in Northern Ireland, both require services to be accessible to people with disabilities. Statutory codes of practice from the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) confirm that websites are considered to be a service, regardless of whether a charge is made for accessing content or not.

Accessibility is good for business

At its heart, accessibility is about enabling more people to use the website. Sky's website drives revenue, promotes services, and spreads a message. Accessibility can increase audience by as much as 17%.

Accessibility boosts search engine performance

Accessible websites can be indexed more efficiently by search engines. Audio, video and graphical content can't be understood by a search engine, but content in text format can. Providing alternative text content (descriptions, transcripts and captions) is one of the cornerstones of good accessibility, and it also gives search engines much more to work with.

Accessibility increases brand loyalty

When someone finds a website easy to use and accessible, they'll often become a loyal visitor. More importantly, they'll often recommend the site to people they know. In marketing terms, personal recommendations are an extremely powerful tool, and accessibility can certainly contribute to that goal.

How is web accessibility measured?

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0 are the globally recognised benchmark for building accessible websites and measuring web accessibility. Sky's website can achieve three different levels of accessibility under these guidelines:

  • Level A, the most basic level;
  • Level AA, the intermediate level;
  • Level AAA, the highest level.

There are various methods of testing a website for accessibility, although none of these methods are perfect.

Usability testing with people who have disabilities.
Usability testing with people who have disabilities can find a great variety of issues, including many that affect the general population. However, you can only cover a few journeys in a test session, so covering all of a large site is impractical.
Usability testing (general population)
Being usable is essentially a pre-cursor to being accessible. Issues that affect everyone will affect people with disabilies, probably to a greater degree.
Manual audit
Assessing web pages against the WCAG guidelines should highlight all accessibility related issues, however, it takes 1-3 hours per page to do properly, so a sample of pages should be selected for auditing. If the pages are representative, the results and recommendations can be applied across the whole site.
Automated testing
Testing with an automated program can cover a great deal of a website, but only for a small proportion of issues. For example, it can say that alternative text for an image is missing, but it cannot tell that a picture of a dog has an alt text "cat".

Nomensa recommend using a combination of methods depending on the project, usually starting with a manual audit and including testing with people who have disabilities when possible.

Public sector organisations are required to meet Level AA, and this is a reasonable and realistic goal for most websites regardless of sector. Whereas Level A doesn't offer the all round experience needed by people with some disabilities, Level AAA introduces challenges that require additional planning (and possibly budget). Sky's accessibility policy (Word document) outlines Level AA as the target.

Final thoughts

No matter what you're working on, you'll be able to find the guidance you need to build accessibility into your project.
